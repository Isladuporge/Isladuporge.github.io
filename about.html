My research focuses on improving wildlife detection and tracking techniques to provide more detailed insights into species abundance, habitat use, and collective behavior. While my work primarily centers on social migratory species, it remains taxonomically broad. I have had the opportunity to work with a variety of terrestrial and marine mammals across diverse biomes, including elephants, baboons, and narwhals.

I hold a Ph.D. in Zoology from the University of Oxford and have conducted research for various government agencies, such as the United States Army Research Office, the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime program on combatting wildlife and forest crime, the government of Saudi Arabia, and Fisheries and Oceans Canada. My research primarily employs satellite and UAV monitoring combined with machine learning techniques but I have also conducted manned aircraft surveys from helicopter and twin otters in the Arctic. Additionally, I have worked with acoustic and biological sampling methodologies, including geophones, hydrophones, and eDNA sampling. I am a certified multicopter and fixed-wing UAV pilot, as well as an advanced nitrox-certified deep-water diver. Since 2017, I have been an 'Explorer' with the National Geographic Society.

I am currently a postdoctoral research associate in the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Princeton University.
