My research is taxonomically broad and driven by methodological innovation to improve wildlife detection and tracking techniques with a focus on socially migratory species. I have had the opportunity to work with a wide range of terrestrial and marine mammals across diverse biomes.

I hold a Ph.D. in Zoology from the University of Oxford and have conducted research for various government agencies, including the United States Army Research Office, the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime program on combating wildlife and forest crime, the government of Saudi Arabia, and the Department of Fisheries and Oceans Canada. My research primarily focuses on advancing techniques that use remote sensing satellite imagery and UAV monitoring combined with machine learning techniques. However, due to the flexibility of methods required to adequately track wildlife at different spatial scales, I have also worked on aerial surveys (helicopters and Twin Otters in the Arctic), sub-aquatic missions including diving and submersibles (Saudi Arabia), and applied acoustic and biological sampling with geophones, hydrophones, and eDNA.

I am a commercially certified operator of multicopter and fixed-wing UAVs, as well as an advanced nitrox-certified deep-water diver. Since 2017, I have been an 'Explorer' with the National Geographic Society. I am currently a postdoctoral research associate in the Department of Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Princeton University - Institutional Webpage.
